Key Findings — What This Data Tells Us
🔍 6 Things We Learned From 22,768 Students
  • 1Nearly 4 in 5 students (78.4%) use ChatGPT — making it one of the most widely adopted AI tools in higher education globally.
  • 2Social influence is a powerful driver. In Latin American countries like Ecuador (61%) and Chile (60%), students were especially likely to try ChatGPT because their friends were using it — a phenomenon called "mimetic adoption."
  • 3Students from Egypt, Tanzania, and Ghana rank among the most AI-confident and career-optimistic — showing that enthusiasm for AI isn't limited to wealthier nations.
  • 466% of students gave ChatGPT a quality rating of 4 or 5 out of 5 — meaning most students who use it find its answers genuinely good or excellent.
  • 5Male students score slightly higher on AI confidence (3.42 vs 3.29 for female), but all gender groups view AI positively overall — the difference is small and all groups score above 3 out of 5.
  • 6Japanese-speaking students stand out with the lowest peer-influence rate (31%) and lowest AI confidence — suggesting a more independent and cautious approach to adopting AI tools in Japan.

Who Uses It & What Drives Adoption
👤 Do Men and Women Feel Differently About AI?
Comparison of two key attitudes across gender groups, both scored 1–5: AI Confidence = how skilled students feel using AI tools; Career Belief = how strongly they think AI skills improve their job prospects.
💡All groups score above 3 out of 5 — everyone has broadly positive attitudes toward AI. Differences between groups are small (less than 0.2 points).
🫂 How Many Students Were Pushed Into AI by Their Friends?
This shows the percentage of students in each world region who say their primary reason for trying ChatGPT was peer influence — seeing friends and classmates use it. This is called "mimetic behavior" (copying others).
💡Spanish-speaking and Hebrew-speaking regions top the chart at ~58%. Japanese students are the most independent at just 31%.
